# Science — Passage Types & Figure Strategy
 
## The 3 Passage Types
 
### 1. Data Representation (2–3 passages per test)
- Charts, graphs, tables with 5–6 questions
- **Strategy:** Go to the figure BEFORE reading the passage text
- Questions test: reading values, identifying trends, comparing data sets, extrapolating
### 2. Research Summary (2–3 passages per test)
- Describes 2–4 experiments with results — 6 questions
- **Strategy:** Read what each experiment tested, then go to figures
- Questions test: experimental design, what variables were controlled, what the results show
### 3. Conflicting Viewpoints (1 passage per test)
- 2–3 scientists or students presenting different explanations — 7 questions
- **Strategy:** Read ALL viewpoints completely before answering ANY questions
- Questions test: understanding each position, comparing positions, identifying what each would agree/disagree with
---
 
## Figure-First Strategy
 
For data representation and research summary:
 
1. **Scan the title** of each figure — what is being measured?
2. **Read axis labels** — what are the units? What do x and y represent?
3. **Identify the trend** — increasing, decreasing, no pattern?
4. **Note the scale** — are the differences large or small relative to the range?
5. **THEN** answer questions by going back to specific figures
**Test yourself:** Can you answer the question using only the figure? If yes, do it. If no, find the one sentence in the passage that helps.
 
---
 
## For Each Question, Ask:
- Which figure does this question refer to? (Look for figure numbers in the question)
- What exact value or trend am I looking for?
- Am I comparing across figures or within one figure?
---
 
## Conflicting Viewpoints Deep Strategy
 
This passage type is unique — it's the most reading-intensive Science passage.
 
**Before questions:**
- Summarize each viewpoint in one sentence: *"Scientist 1 believes X because Y."*
- Note where they agree (often they share some assumptions)
- Note where they disagree (this is what most questions test)
**Question types:**
- *"According to Scientist 1..."* → find the exact claim in Scientist 1's section
- *"Scientist 2 would most likely respond to Scientist 1's claim by..."* → apply Scientist 2's core position
- *"Both scientists would agree that..."* → find something neither disputes
- *"Which additional finding would support Scientist 1's hypothesis?"* → ask: what would make Scientist 1's explanation more likely?
---
 
## Common Science Traps
 
| Trap | How to avoid |
|------|-------------|
| Misreading axis units | Always double-check what the y-axis measures |
| Confusing two similar graphs | Read the figure title before each question |
| Answering from outside knowledge | ACT Science never requires science knowledge — everything is in the passage |
| Rushing through conflicting viewpoints | This passage rewards slow careful reading — budget 9 min |
| Picking extreme answer choices | "Proves," "completely disproves," "always" — almost always wrong |
 
---
 
## Science Does NOT Require Science Knowledge
 
This is the most important rule. Every answer is in the passage or figures. If you think you need to remember a biology or chemistry fact, you're looking at the wrong place. Return to the text or figure.
