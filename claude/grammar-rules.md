# English — Grammar Rules Quick Reference
 
## Punctuation
 
### Commas
- After introductory clauses/phrases: *Although he studied, he felt nervous.*
- Before coordinating conjunctions joining independent clauses (FANBOYS): *She studied, and she improved.*
- Around non-essential (non-restrictive) clauses: *Alex, who scored a 31, aims for a 36.*
- No comma between subject and verb
- No comma before "that" clauses
### Semicolons
- Join two independent clauses (both sides must be complete sentences)
- Before conjunctive adverbs: *She studied hard; however, she still felt nervous.*
- Never use a semicolon before a dependent clause
### Colons
- Introduce a list, explanation, or elaboration — the left side must be a complete sentence
- *He had one goal: a 36.*
### Dashes
- Em dash sets off a parenthetical or adds emphasis — stronger than commas
- Pair them: if you open with a dash, close with a dash
### Apostrophes
- Possession: *Alex's score* (singular), *students' scores* (plural ending in s)
- Contractions: it's = it is; who's = who is; they're = they are
- No apostrophe in possessive pronouns: its, whose, their, your
---
 
## Pronouns
 
### Agreement
- Pronoun must agree in number with its antecedent
- Singular indefinite pronouns: each, every, either, neither, anyone, someone, everyone, no one → use singular pronoun
- *Everyone should bring **his or her** ID.*
### Case
- Subject pronouns: I, he, she, they, we, who
- Object pronouns: me, him, her, them, us, whom
- Test: remove the other noun → *Between [him] and me, not between he and I*
### Ambiguous reference
- A pronoun must clearly refer to one antecedent. If "it" or "they" could refer to two things, the sentence must be rewritten.
---
 
## Modifiers
 
### Dangling modifiers
- The modifier must describe the subject of the main clause
- Wrong: *Running to school, the rain soaked her.* (rain wasn't running)
- Right: *Running to school, she got soaked by the rain.*
### Misplaced modifiers
- Keep modifying phrases next to what they modify
- Wrong: *She only ate vegetables.* (only modifies ate — she didn't do anything else?)
- Right: *She ate only vegetables.*
---
 
## Subject-Verb Agreement
 
- Find the true subject (ignore prepositional phrases between subject and verb)
- *The box of chocolates **is** on the table.*
- Collective nouns (team, group, committee) → singular verb in American English
- Subjects joined by "or/nor" → verb agrees with the closer subject
- *Neither the students nor the teacher **was** ready.*
---
 
## Parallelism
 
- Items in a list must be in the same grammatical form
- Wrong: *She likes running, to swim, and hike.*
- Right: *She likes running, swimming, and hiking.*
- Comparisons must compare like things: *Alex's score is higher than **that of** their classmate.*
---
 
## Verb Tense
 
- Maintain consistent tense throughout a passage unless there is a logical time shift
- Past perfect (*had done*) = action completed before another past action
- Present perfect (*has done*) = action started in the past, still relevant now
- Watch for sequence markers: *before, after, when, by the time*
---
 
## Common Traps on the ACT
 
| Trap | Wrong | Right |
|------|-------|-------|
| Its vs it's | It's cover is torn | Its cover is torn |
| Then vs than | Better then expected | Better than expected |
| Affect vs effect | The affect was huge | The effect was huge |
| Who vs whom | Who did she call? | Whom did she call? |
| Less vs fewer | Less students | Fewer students |
| Amount vs number | A large amount of people | A large number of people |

## Enhanced ACT Updates

### Singular They (accepted)
"Everyone did their best" = correct on
Enhanced ACT. Singular "they/their" is
preferred over "his or her" for indefinite
pronouns (anyone, everyone, each, someone,
neither) when both are grammatically valid.
"His or her" = wordy trap on Enhanced ACT.

### DELETE/OMIT Rule
When an answer offers to DELETE the
underlined portion, test it first.
It is correct a high percentage of the time
on the ACT because the test prioritizes
concision.
