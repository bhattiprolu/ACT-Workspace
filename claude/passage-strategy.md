# Reading — Passage Strategy Guide
 
## The 4 Passage Types
 
| Type | Description | Difficulty (for most students) | Key skill |
|------|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------|
| Literary narrative | Fiction or memoir excerpt | Medium | Tone, character motivation |
| Social science | History, anthropology, sociology | Medium | Author's argument, evidence |
| Humanities | Art, music, culture, philosophy | Medium-Hard | Author's perspective, interpretation |
| Natural science | Biology, chemistry, physics | Hard | Technical detail, data |
 
**Determine your optimal order by Week 2.** Most students do best starting with their strongest passage type and saving hardest for last — but with only 8–9 min per passage, order matters.
 
---
 
## Time Budget
 
- **Total:** 35 minutes for 4 passages (10 questions each)
- **Per passage:** 8–9 minutes
  - 3 min reading
  - 5–6 min answering (35–40 sec/question)
- **If stuck on a question:** mark it, move on, return in the last 60–90 seconds
---
 
## The Reading Approach
 
### Step 1 — Read the passage actively (3 min)
- Read for the BIG PICTURE: what is the author's main point or purpose?
- Mark: character names, dates, location shifts (fiction); key claims, evidence structure (nonfiction)
- Don't memorize details — you'll go back for them
### Step 2 — Answer questions strategically
- **Do NOT answer from memory** — always return to the passage for anything other than the main idea
- For detail questions: go to the line, read 3 lines above and below
- For inference questions: find the evidence first, then eliminate wrong answers
- For "author's purpose" questions: ask *why* the author included this — not just *what* it says
---
 
## Question Types and Strategies
 
### Main idea / primary purpose
- What is the passage as a whole doing?
- Wrong answers: too narrow (about one detail), too broad (beyond the passage), or distort the tone
- The correct answer uses language like "argues," "explores," "describes," "recounts" — match the verb to what the passage actually does
### Detail questions ("According to the passage…")
- These are gifts — the answer is in the text
- Find the line before answering
- Wrong answers often use true details from the wrong part of the passage
### Inference questions ("It can be reasonably inferred…")
- The answer is not stated — but it must be provable from the text
- Find the textual evidence first, then evaluate answer choices
- Wrong answers: too extreme ("always," "never," "proves"), outside the passage, or require outside knowledge
### Vocabulary in context ("As used in line X, the word Y most nearly means…")
- Go to the line and read the full sentence
- Substitute each answer choice — which makes the most sense in context?
- The correct answer is rarely the most common definition of the word
### Author's purpose / function ("The author mentions X in order to…")
- Ask: what job does this detail do in the passage?
- Does it support a claim? Provide contrast? Introduce a new idea? Emphasize a point?
- Wrong answers describe what the sentence says, not what it does
### Comparative / paired passage questions (if applicable)
- Answer single-passage questions first (they're clearly labeled)
- For "both authors" questions: find agreement by elimination — if either author clearly disagrees, cut that choice
- For "Author 2 would respond to Author 1 by…" — find Author 2's core position, then apply it
---
 
## Elimination Strategy
 
For every wrong answer, identify WHY it's wrong:
1. **Too extreme** — uses "always," "never," "completely," "proven"
2. **Out of scope** — uses information not in the passage
3. **Distorts the text** — uses real words from the passage but twists the meaning
4. **True but wrong question** — accurate about the passage but answers a different question
5. **Right details, wrong location** — pulls a real detail from the wrong paragraph

## Passage Type Notes
 
### Literary narrative
- Track: who is narrating, what is the central tension, how does the narrator's attitude shift?
- Common questions: narrator's tone, character's motivation, effect of a specific detail
### Social science
- Track: what is the author's central argument, what evidence supports it?
- Common questions: main idea, author's purpose, what evidence supports X claim
### Humanities
- Track: author's perspective on the subject (positive, critical, ambivalent?)
- Common questions: author's attitude, purpose of an example, vocabulary in context
### Natural science
- Track: what phenomenon is being described, what caused it, what were the results?
- Common questions: detail retrieval, sequence of events, what a term means in context
- Strategy: don't get intimidated by technical terms — the answer is always in the passage


## Reading Passage Format (locked Jun 21)
Source: Official ACT Prep Guide 2025|2026

PASSAGE LENGTH: 80–90 lines, verified
before every delivery. No shorter passages.

QUESTION DISTRIBUTION per passage (9 Q):
  2–3 max explicit line references
  2–3 inference questions
  1   main idea/purpose
  1   vocab in context
  1   function/author's purpose
  1   POV/narrator (Lit Narrative only)
  1   character/motivation (Lit Narrative)

PASSAGE TYPES:
  Passage 1: Literary Narrative
  Passage 2: Social Science
  Passage 3: Humanities
  Passage 4: Natural Science

PACING: 67 sec/Q · ~10 min/passage
  ~3 min reading · ~7 min questions
  Do strongest passage FIRST

SIX TRAP TYPES (cheat sheet):
  1. Partial truth — correct detail,
     wrong scope
  2. Opposite — flips author's position
  3. Outside passage — true but not stated
  4. Extreme language — always/never/all
  5. Almost right — matches 90%, misses
     key qualifier
  6. Right answer, wrong question — true
     but doesn't answer what was asked

TWISTED QUALIFIER RULE:
  "complete/entirely/always/never" in an
  inference answer = almost always wrong.
  Passage tone is almost always nuanced —
  never absolute.
